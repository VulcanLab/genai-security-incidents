01

Abstract

As AI systems increasingly take on tasks whose failures cause real-world loss, understanding which AI threats and risks are insurable has become a central question for the firms that deploy them and the carriers that underwrite that exposure. Our prior work mapped 55 AI threat classes against 26 insurance products and exclusion regimes, but coded coverage from public carrier materials only and explicitly reported no claim-frequency or severity data. This companion paper supplies the missing empirical layer. We join the 55-threat catalog to 3,054 recent real-world AI incidents (2024–2026), mapped, where a threat predicate applies, to the 55 threat classes of the original insurability matrix via a documented, reproducible procedure.

We then ask a question the positioning matrix could not: does affirmative coverage track observed incident evidence? It does not. Affirmative coverage breadth bears little relation to how often a peril is recently observed, and the market's most decisive coverage choices run directly against incidence. Model drift, the headline peril of the oldest affirmative product, has zero real-world incidents in the corpus yet draws seven affirmative coverage cells, while deepfake fraud is the single most frequent recent peril yet draws only three affirmative cells, and shadow AI shows high incidence and severity with no affirmative cover and two exclusions. We organize threats into a coverage–incidence frontier with four quadrants (covered, exposure gap, anticipatory, latent) and use it to locate the market's sharpest mismatches. We stress that an incident corpus is not insured-loss experience; it grounds threat prevalence and severity, not claim frequency or loss cost.

Keywords: AI insurance · insurability frontier · incident data · threat prevalence · silent-AI exposure · coverage gap · foundation model concentration · systemic risk

The evidence, interactively

All figures are computed live from the released incident dataset joined to the coverage matrix of the companion paper. Hover for per-threat detail.

Fig. 1 — Top-18 threats by recent (2024–26) real-world incidence, colored by coverage–incidence quadrant. Deepfake fraud (T-35) leads; shadow AI (T-37) stands out: high incidence, no affirmative cover, active exclusions.

What it measures

For each threat class, the number of publicly documented real-world incidents (2024–2026) whose record satisfies that threat's predicate — the top 18 of 55 shown here. An incident may map to more than one threat, so bars are prevalence per threat, not a partition of the corpus.

Why it matters

Observed incidence is highly skewed, and a positioning matrix contains no incidence information: this is where loss-generating events actually cluster. The identity of the leader is itself a finding — the dominant AI peril is a misuse and fraud event, not a model-internal failure.

The two code-execution rows (T-05, T-15) are the AI supply-side security tail: remote code execution in AI development tooling (GitHub Copilot, Claude Desktop, Cursor and similar), insurance-relevant under technology E&O and cyber but distinct from user-facing AI harm.

Takeaway

Most of the tallest bars are yellow — high-incidence perils sitting in the exposure-gap quadrant. The user-facing AI-harm leaders are deepfake fraud (609), regulatory non-compliance (424), hallucination (132), prompt injection (116), and bias and fairness failures (107).

Fig. 2 — The coverage–incidence frontier: observed real-world incidence (log scale) against affirmative coverage breadth, split at the medians (incidence = 5, affirmative cells = 5; a threat lying exactly at a median falls in the lower group). The lower-right is the exposure-gap zone: frequent but thinly covered. The upper-left is anticipatory cover: broadly covered but rarely observed.

What it measures

Each of the 55 threats on two axes at once: how often it is observed (realized incidents, log scale) and how broadly the market affirmatively covers it (count of “A” cells across the 26 products and exclusion regimes of the companion matrix). Dashed lines mark the medians of both distributions.

Why it matters

This is the paper's central test: if the market is sorting risk by peril, is it sorting on the perils that actually occur? A coverage map shows where the market says risk sits; an incidence map shows where events actually cluster. How far the two diverge is the finding.

Takeaway

Position on one axis tells us little about position on the other — the AI-insurance market is not yet an incidence-driven market. Model drift (T-29, top-left) is the purest anticipatory case: zero recorded incidents, seven affirmative cells. Shadow AI (T-37, bottom-right) is the mirror image: 28 incidents, 75% critical-or-high severity, no affirmative cover and two exclusions.

One caveat: several high-incidence perils sit exactly at five affirmative cells, a boundary case — and they may carry silent coverage under standard professional-indemnity wordings that an affirmative cell count does not capture.

Fig. 3 — Evidence maturity by threat (2024–2026): realized real-world incidents, research/red-team demonstrations, and disclosed attack surface, stacked. Streams are distinct evidence modalities and are not summed into a single frequency. Of the 55 threats, 42 are Realized, 3 Demonstrated, 2 Surfacing, and 8 Theoretical.

What it measures

Three kinds of evidence per threat, kept distinct: realized field incidents (black), demonstrated feasibility from research and red-team work (dark yellow), and surfacing attack surface from vulnerability disclosures (yellow). Each threat is classified by the strongest evidence available; a fourth class, theoretical, has no evidence in any stream.

Why it matters

The composition explains the anticipatory quadrant of Fig. 2: perils that are broadly covered yet rarely realized often carry substantial demonstrated evidence — RAG poisoning shows 11 demonstrations against 3 field incidents, membership inference 3 demonstrations and none. Anticipatory cover is tracking feasibility established in the literature, not pricing perils with no basis.

Takeaway

The disclosure stream reveals a forming attack surface ahead of losses: MCP server compromise has 18 realized incidents but 123 disclosed vulnerabilities; unauthorized MCP exposure shows 47 disclosures against 5 field incidents. These surfacing-heavy perils — where attack surface accumulates faster than realized losses — are the leading edge an underwriter would watch.

The dataset

Incident-level records underlying the paper: publicly disclosed GenAI / agentic-AI security incidents, vulnerabilities, and red-team findings, tagged against common AI-security taxonomies (OWASP LLM Top 10, OWASP ASI, MITRE ATLAS, NIST AI RMF) and crosswalked to the threat IDs of the Insurability Frontier companion paper. 4,328 records · 2024–2026 · data cutoff 17 May 2026. A subset of GenAI & Agentic AI Security Incidents (Zenodo v2.0.0).

Dataset (CSV)
CLI-friendly export (UTF-8, RFC-4180 quoted). 20 columns incl. stream, severity, CVSS, taxonomy tags, and the T-01…T-55 threat crosswalk.
Dataset (XLSX)
Canonical workbook with an embedded Readme sheet plus the Incidents 2024–2026 data sheet.
Data dictionary →
Column-by-column documentation, evidence-stream definitions, and quick-start snippets in the repository README.

Read the numbers with care

  • This is incident data, not insured-loss experience. Counts reflect documented public disclosures, not claims or losses; there is no exposure base and no loss-development triangle.
  • Streams are distinct evidence types and are not summed. Realized, Demonstrated, and Surfacing measure different things.
  • The threat mapping is heuristic. A record maps to a threat only when its documented predicate is satisfied; each per-threat count is a best-evidence estimate, not a census.
